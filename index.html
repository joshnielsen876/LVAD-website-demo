Compare Options

LVAD

An LVAD is a mechanical pump that supports the left ventricle so blood can circulate when the heart is too weak. It can be used as a bridge to transplant or as a long-term therapy.

Timing. Therapy can be available sooner because it does not depend on a donor organ.
Impact on life. Device management is required, including carrying a controller and batteries and caring for the driveline site.
Risks. Bleeding, infection, and stroke are possible. Early rehospitalization is common.
Show practical notes
  • Showering is possible with center-specific routines. Swimming is not recommended because components cannot be submerged.
  • Travel is feasible with planning for power, backup equipment, and clinic contacts.
  • Regular clinic visits and anticoagulation monitoring are typical in the first months.

Heart Transplant

A transplant removes the failing heart and replaces it with a donor heart. It offers the most device-free lifestyle for eligible patients after recovery and lifelong follow-up.

Long-term goals. Many patients return to daily activities with few physical restrictions once recovery is complete.
Waiting. Eligibility and donor availability determine the timing. Some patients wait months.
Medicines. Lifelong immunosuppression is required and raises infection risk. Regular lab tests and biopsies are common in the first year.
Show practical notes
  • Vaccinations and infection precautions are important because of immunosuppression.
  • Follow-up visits are frequent in the first months and then spread out.
  • A strong caregiver network supports appointments and medications.

Reminder: Only your care team can advise which option fits your medical situation.

Questions and Concerns

About LVADs

How will I manage power and equipment?

You will carry a controller and batteries and will learn simple routines for charging and swapping power. Your team provides training and a 24 hour contact.

Can I shower or bathe?

Centers provide shower plans that protect components. Soaking or swimming is not recommended because the device has external parts.

What complications should I know about?

Bleeding, infection at the driveline, and stroke are possible. Your team will show you how to reduce risks and when to call.

About Transplant

How long could I wait?

Waiting time depends on your medical urgency, blood type, size, and local donor availability. Your team will update you on status and options.

What is rejection and how is it monitored?

Your immune system can attack the donor heart. Medicines reduce this risk. Clinics monitor with labs, imaging, and biopsies in the first year.

What lifestyle changes are needed?

Medicines are daily and infection precautions are important. Most patients resume many activities once recovery is complete.

Frequently Asked

Is either option a cure?

Both options are treatments that improve survival and quality of life. They require lifelong follow-up and a partnership with your care team.

Can I travel?

Travel is possible after both therapies. LVAD travel requires power planning and spare components. Transplant recipients plan around medicine timing and clinic schedules.

Who helps me learn the routines?

LVAD programs provide structured training and practice before discharge. Transplant programs provide medication teaching and infection prevention guidance.
